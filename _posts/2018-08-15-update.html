The history of the teaching machine often focuses on two devices – or rather, on two psychologists who invented them: Sidney Pressey and B. F. Skinner. The story of these mechanical devices is told often as simply the prelude to computer-assisted instruction. “Computers are now much better teaching machines,” Skinner himself admitted in Science in 1989 (although he added that most of CAI does it wrong).

As I plan to focus on the mechanical teaching machines, not the later digital ones, I thought I’d write down a longer list of devices and start to collect images, pricing, description. (I have created a separate page here on this website which I will update as my research continues.)

This initial list is drawn from “A Survey of Auto-Instructional Devices” written by Felix Kopstein and Isabel Shillestad of the USAF’s Aeronautical Systems Division and published in September 1961. The categories for these machines are theirs: Skinner’s, Pressey’s, Norman Crowder’s, and Gordon Pask’s formats. They too end their survey with a look at the computer as a teaching machine.

Kopstein and Shillestad note that their list is not exhaustive, and I think there are some that I might add (and perhaps some I might remove) as I move forward on this book project. (I do feel duty-bound, as a Spencer fellow, to include Lyle Spencer’s SRA cards.)
